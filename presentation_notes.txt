Recommended 7-minute structure
Slide 1 — Hook: make it human

Title: What if the world’s warning signals weren’t designed for you?

Start with your teammate.

What to say:

One of our teammates is red-green colorblind.
Which sounds manageable, until you realise how much of modern life relies on color-coded information.
Traffic lights. Road signs. Grocery shopping. Alerts. Indicators.
At night, especially, red on dark backgrounds can become really hard to distinguish.
So this project started very simply: not as “let’s build some AI thing,” but as “why is basic visual information still inaccessible in 2026?”

Funny/light line:

Basically, we discovered that the world is running a very aggressive red-green subscription plan.

Why this works:

immediately nails problem identification
makes the problem personal and memorable
avoids sounding like a generic hackathon intro
Slide 2 — The problem and why it matters now

Title: The problem isn’t color. It’s missed information.

What to say:

Colorblindness is often treated like a minor inconvenience.
But the real issue is not seeing “the wrong color” — it’s missing information that other people get instantly.
That can mean hesitation while driving, uncertainty while shopping, or needing to double-check things others process in a glance.
And today is the right time to solve this because real-time computer vision, lightweight mobile inference, and AR wearables have finally made live visual assistance practical — not theoretical.

Frame the pain points as 3 buckets:

Safety: traffic lights, road signs, night visibility
Daily independence: shopping, item recognition, ripeness, labels
Confidence: reducing second-guessing in everyday environments

Funny/light line:

Nobody wants to play “guess the banana” in aisle 4.

This hits the rubric’s “why is today the right time” and “urgency/frequency” really well.

Slide 3 — How we validated it

Title: We didn’t guess. We asked.

This should be one of your strongest slides because customer validation is worth 15 points, which is huge.

What to say:

On Day 1, before building anything, we focused on ideation and customer validation.
We interviewed multiple people individually and asked three things:

Would this actually be useful?
In which situations would you trust or use it?
What features would make it genuinely worth trying?

Then summarise findings clearly:

users were most interested in driving/night visibility
shopping and daily object recognition also came up often
people cared about speed and simplicity, not a cluttered interface
they preferred assistive overlays over complicated settings
trust mattered: users wanted help identifying things, not constant noise

Strong phrasing:

The biggest insight was that users didn’t want “more information.”
They wanted the right information, at the right time, with almost no friction.

Funny/light line:

In other words, no one asked for a 14-button dashboard that requires a PhD to turn on.

If you have even rough numbers, include them:

“We interviewed X people”
“Y% mentioned driving/night visibility”
“Z% said they’d try it”

Even if it’s small sample size, that’s still better than vague claims.

Slide 4 — What we decided to build

Title: From feedback to product direction

What to say:

Based on that feedback, we narrowed the MVP to two core functions:

live color remapping for difficult color combinations, like red/blue and yellow/cyan
road sign detection to highlight important visual cues during driving

This gave us a focused first product: an accessibility assistant for real-world interpretation.

Then explain the product simply:

camera sees the world
app shifts difficult colors into more distinguishable ranges
object detection identifies likely road signs
important elements are highlighted clearly
future version overlays this onto Meta Ray-Ban glasses in AR

Better phrasing than “Google Lens for colorblind people”:

You can think of it as a real-time accessibility layer for vision.

That sounds more original and more defensible.

Slide 5 — Why this is different

Title: Why existing tools weren’t enough

This is where you cover USP + defensibility.

What to say:

There are color filters.
There are object detectors.
There are general camera apps.
But we found a gap: most tools do one piece in isolation.
We’re combining personalized visual reinterpretation with context-aware detection in a way that directly serves colorblind users in real-world settings.

Then make the differentiation explicit:

Existing alternatives

static filters
non-specialized accessibility settings
general-purpose visual search tools

Your edge

built specifically for colorblind use cases
focused on high-stakes moments like driving at night
combines perception correction + scene understanding
designed for future AR wearable deployment

Strong line:

We’re not just recoloring the world. We’re helping users understand what in the world matters.

That is a good pitch sentence.

Slide 6 — Our process across the 3 days

Title: How we went from idea to prototype

This should be very chronological and polished.

What to say:

We structured the hackathon like a compressed startup sprint.

Day 1
identified the problem through personal experience
brainstormed use cases
conducted customer interviews
narrowed down the most valuable features
Day 2
built the MVP pipeline
implemented live color transformation
trained a neural network / object detection model for road sign recognition
tested feasibility of combining both in one workflow
Day 3
refining the prototype
integrating road sign detection into the app
improving usability and performance
shaping the product story and presentation around what users actually valued

Funny/light line:

Day 1 was empathy. Day 2 was caffeine. Day 3 was integration bugs.

This slide matters because judges want process, not just outcome.

Slide 7 — The prototype

Title: What the MVP does today

This should map directly to Implementation and Value delivery.

What to say:

Our MVP currently demonstrates two key capabilities:

real-time color shifting to make difficult hues more distinguishable
road sign detection to highlight critical cues in the environment

Then explain value through scenarios:

Night driving: improve visibility of important signs and signals
Shopping: distinguish produce and visually similar items more confidently
Daily navigation: reduce uncertainty in color-dependent environments

Important wording:

The goal of the MVP is not to solve every visual challenge at once.
It is to prove that real-time assistive interpretation is both feasible and useful.

That sounds mature and realistic.

Slide 8 — Demo framing

Title: What we’re testing through the prototype

This is subtle but very smart, because the rubric explicitly asks how the value and assumptions will be tested through the build.

Say this:

Our prototype is designed to test three key assumptions:

that live color remapping improves practical distinguishability
that object detection adds value in high-risk contexts like driving
that users prefer an assistive, low-friction interface over manual tools

Then mention what feedback you’re looking for:

Is it fast enough?
Is it intuitive?
Does it reduce uncertainty?
Which contexts feel most valuable?

This makes you sound like founders, not just builders.

Slide 9 — Why AR glasses make sense

Title: Why this belongs on smart glasses

What to say:

We see the long-term home for this product on AR wearables like Meta Ray-Ban glasses.
Why? Because accessibility works best when it disappears into the user’s routine.
People don’t want to stop, unlock an app, point a camera, and wait.
They want assistance in the moment they need it — hands-free, fast, and natural.

Then explain why glasses are strong:

always-on camera context
overlays directly in field of view
safer and less distracting than checking a phone
strong fit for navigation and mobility

Funny/light line:

Also, if the future insists on putting cameras on our faces, we might as well make them useful.

Slide 10 — Business case

Title: Why people would actually pay for this

Keep this brief. Business Case is only 10 points, so don’t overdo it.

What to say:

We see a few possible monetization paths:

a freemium mobile app with premium assistive modes
partnerships with smart-glasses platforms
accessibility collaborations with driving, navigation, or retail partners
potentially B2B or institutional distribution through accessibility organisations

Then say demand is validated through:

interviews
strong emotional resonance of the problem
repeated interest in real-world use cases like driving and shopping

Funny/light line:

And when it comes to monetisation… this is the part where we say “investors, please don’t be shy.”

Or:

This is where you help us. Ideally with money.

That’ll land if delivered dry.

Slide 11 — Risks and how you thought about them

Title: What could go wrong, and how we’re approaching it

This helps with market trends & risks and makes you sound grounded.

Mention 3 risks:

False detections or missed detections
→ mitigate through better training, confidence thresholds, and careful UX
Too much visual clutter
→ keep overlays minimal and context-aware
Trust and safety in driving contexts
→ position as assistive support, not autonomous decision-making

Strong line:

We’re not claiming this replaces human judgment.
We’re building a tool that reduces ambiguity where ambiguity already exists.

Very important. Makes your pitch sound responsible.

Slide 12 — End with vision

Title: What we’re building toward

What to say:

We started with one teammate’s lived experience.
Over three days, that turned into interviews, insights, a prototype, and a clearer product direction.
What we have now is not just a demo. It’s the beginning of a new way to think about accessibility:
not as an afterthought, but as a real-time intelligent layer between people and the world around them.

Then finish strong:

If the world communicates through color, signs, and visual cues, then everyone deserves access to that information.
We’re building that access.

Funny closer option:

And ideally, we’re also saving bananas, traffic signs, and team members from future misunderstandings.
